I am Jou Fei Huang, a Taiwanese researcher and assistant professor at the Graduate Institute of National Policy and Public Affairs, National Chung Hsing University (NCHU).

My research lies at the intersection of comparative politics and development studies, with a focus on foreign aid, infrastructure and energy supply, and collective action — specifically, how foreign aid diminishes or increases the cost of protest mobilization. My work is structured around three main strands: (1) the variation in types of public goods in reducing cost for collective action and how this shapes leaders' allocation decisions; (2) the conditions under which governments address the negative externalities of public goods provision; and (3) the influence of foreign donors as public goods providers on state legitimacy and public support for democracy.

The first strand of my research builds on my dissertation, which examines the interplay between public goods typologies and political institutions. Traditional perspectives suggest that democracies provide more public goods than autocracies. However, recent studies challenge this assumption, arguing that democratization does not necessarily lead to an increase in public goods provision if former autocratic regimes had already established widespread public services. These debates motivate my inquiry into the variation in public goods provision across autocracies, particularly in developing countries. Given the expansion of modern autocracies and the life cycle of public goods — where maintenance costs decline over time — it is essential to reassess institutional influences on public goods provision.

My research addresses two key questions: (1) When and to what extent do the types of public goods matter? (2) Under what conditions do mass threats influence leaders' willingness to provide collaborative public goods, which I define as those that lower the cost of collective action? By incorporating the concept of collaboration into public goods typology, I find that while autocrats and democratic leaders allocate similar levels of resources to non-collaborative public goods (e.g., public health), autocrats systematically underinvest in collaborative public goods, such as telecommunications. Furthermore, my dissertation develops a theoretical framework and empirical tests demonstrating how past protest records shape autocratic leaders' incentives to maintain collaborative public goods, resulting in lower nighttime light intensity in regions with higher collective action potential.

Another ongoing project examines the impact of public goods provision by former autocracies on nascent democracies. I find that the likelihood of opposition success is significantly lower when former autocracies provided a diverse public goods portfolio. However, this challenge is more pronounced when an authoritarian successor party remains a competitor in electoral politics.

The second strand of my research investigates the conditions under which governments address the negative externalities of public goods provision. For example, which political regimes are more effective in mitigating road fatalities and road-related CO₂ emissions following infrastructure expansion? In collaboration with Dr. Thong-Hong Lin, we examine why democratic regimes, despite higher vehicle ownership rates and more extensive transportation networks, are more effective in reducing road fatalities. Our findings suggest that democratic institutions mitigate road fatalities more effectively only when women's political empowerment is high (published in Policy Studies). In another collaborative project, we analyze how variations in transportation infrastructure across political regimes explain why democracies lag behind autocracies in reducing transportation-related CO₂ emissions. While democratic leaders prioritize accessibility, autocrats often construct roads for political control and military deployment, leading to lower accessibility despite extensive infrastructure. Consequently, lower accessibility in autocracies paradoxically contributes to reduced transportation-related CO₂ emissions.
